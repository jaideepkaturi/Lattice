Research
Theme 01

Active Transport in Confined Geometries

Chemically active Janus colloids provide a controlled platform for isolating how self-generated solute fields, phoretic slip, hydrodynamic image flows, and steric confinement determine microswimmer transport. In pressure-driven channels, spherical swimmers acquire a deterministic cross-stream orientation through the coupling of near-wall swimming activity to imposed shear, producing migration transverse to streamlines. At structured boundaries, submicron steps, posts, and asymmetric ratchets rectify stochastic active trajectories by aligning particle orientation through topography-mediated phoretic and hydrodynamic interactions. Extending these ideas to liquid–liquid interfaces shows that interfacial alignment and modified propulsion speeds can generate long-lived guidance in geometries relevant to complex fluids and microfluidic transport.

Theme 02

Emergent Collective States

The central question is how simple driven units generate collective states that cannot be inferred from single-particle dynamics. In populations of shape-anisotropic active Janus particles, induced-charge electrophoretic propulsion and hydrodynamically assisted bound-state formation produce sparse, dynamic arrested-motility phases, distinct from dense motility-induced phase separation or passive jamming. In chiral active fluids, rotating magnetic fields convert individual colloidal spin into mesoscale circulation through coupled hydrodynamic and dipolar interactions, generating cohesive clusters with edge currents. Adding light-activated diffusiophoretic interactions provides a reversible control parameter for cluster cohesion, network porosity, and the transition between compact and expanded collective states.

Key publications —

Arrested-motility states · Sci. Adv. 2022

Theme 03

Activity in Structured Media

Structured media introduce orientational elasticity, anisotropic dissipation, and topological constraints that fundamentally alter active transport. Acoustically powered bubble swimmers in lyotropic liquid crystals couple their propulsion to the local nematic director, exhibiting bimodal swimming states parallel or perpendicular to the director while acoustic streaming locally melts order and nucleates trailing defects. At the material scale, ultrasonic forcing of a lyotropic chromonic liquid crystal creates a fully synthetic active nematic: acoustic energy is converted into microscopic extensile stresses that destabilize long-range orientational order, drive undulation instabilities, proliferate topological defects, and produce persistent director-field vortices at high activity.
