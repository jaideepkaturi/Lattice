Research Proposal · Pore-Scale Physics

Asphaltene
Damage

Near-wellbore asphaltene deposition and its recovery in a producing reservoir, rendered at pore scale. Polycyclic aromatic aggregates precipitate when local pressure drops below the thermodynamic onset threshold, progressively restricting pore throats and collapsing near-wellbore permeability. Understanding the pore-scale geometry of this damage — and its dissolution — is the central problem of this research.

Phase I — Open network: reservoir fluid saturates the grain-boundary pore space; connectivity is high and effective permeability isotropic. Phase II — Onset: the local pressure drops below the thermodynamic onset threshold; Flory–Huggins instability drives polycyclic aromatic molecules out of solution onto grain surfaces — deposition most severe in the high-drawdown zone adjacent to the wellbore. Phase III — Constriction: growing surface coatings narrow pore throats; near-wellbore effective permeability collapses to k/k₀ ≈ 0.15, disproportionate to deposit volume because the narrowest throats govern network connectivity. Phase IV — Dissolution front: solvent infiltrates radially from the wellbore face, dissolving surface coatings as the front advances. Phases V–VI — Recovery: throat apertures reopen progressively and permeability is restored as the dissolution front sweeps the near-wellbore zone.
